# Prostate Cancer: A PSA on PSA

**Author:** Nicholas Nelson, Taylor Yeater, Ted Schaeffer, Peter Attia
**Type:** Article
**Published:** 2026-04-18
**Started:** 2026-04-21

---

## Summary

Prostate cancer is the second leading cause of cancer death in men, yet it progresses in a predictable, stepwise fashion — making early detection highly actionable. The 15-year survival rate from first diagnosis is 97%, but this collapses to a 38% five-year survival once the cancer reaches stage IV, with a median survival of 30 months. Despite this, advanced and metastatic diagnoses are rising.

The primary driver is the 2012 USPSTF recommendation against PSA screening, which was built on the deeply flawed PLCO trial. In that trial, 39–64% of the supposed "no screening" control arm reported having had a PSA test within the past year — every single year of the study. When reanalyzed to account for this contamination, the same PLCO data show a 27–32% reduction in prostate cancer mortality attributable to regular PSA testing. The consequences of reduced screening are now measurable: since 2012, US data show a 3.3% per-year increase in stage III diagnoses and a 6.0% per-year increase in stage IV diagnoses.

The modern PSA toolkit is far more sophisticated than the framework the USPSTF evaluated. PSA is best used as a tracked trend rather than a single value: PSA velocity (rate of change over time) and PSA density (PSA concentration ÷ prostate volume via MRI) each dramatically improve risk stratification. MRI now serves as a less invasive intermediary between elevated PSA and biopsy. The 2025 PRIME trial demonstrated that contrast-free biparametric MRI is noninferior to the gadolinium-enhanced multiparametric gold standard (29.2% vs. 29.6% cancer detection rate) while taking half the scan time.

When biopsy is warranted, transperineal (TP) biopsy — entering through the perineum rather than the rectum — achieved a 0% infection rate in the PREVENT trial (1,700 patients), compared to the 5–7% rate for the standard transrectal approach. TP biopsy also improves detection of high-grade tumors and accesses the base and front of the prostate that transrectal approaches tend to miss. Despite this, only 37.2% of US urologists currently perform it.

A critical and underappreciated confound: men taking 5-alpha reductase inhibitors (5-ARIs) like finasteride experience PSA suppression of roughly 50% within 12 months. Raw PSA values must be corrected upward (2x for years 1–2, 2.3x for years 2–7, 2.5x for years 7+) to be interpreted accurately. In a VA cohort, men on finasteride were diagnosed with metastatic prostate cancer more than twice as often as those not on a 5-ARI (6.7% vs. 2.9%) — because clinicians failed to apply this correction.

## Key Ideas

- **PSA screening saves lives, yet USPSTF recommended against it** — trials show routine PSA screening reduces prostate cancer mortality by 44–64%, preventing up to 3.1 metastatic cases per 1,000 men screened. The 2012 guidelines were built on contaminated data.
- **The PLCO trial was fatally contaminated** — 39–64% of the "no screening" control arm underwent PSA testing annually throughout the study. Reanalysis of the same data shows a 27–32% mortality reduction with regular PSA screening.
- **Advanced diagnoses are rising as a direct consequence** — since 2012: +3.3%/year stage III, +6.0%/year stage IV in the USA; +3.7%/year stage IV in Canadian men under 75.
- **PSA as a trend, not a snapshot** — individual values vary by 15% day-to-day and jump 40% after ejaculation. PSA velocity (>0.35 ng/mL/year at baseline PSA <4.0 ng/mL) is the clinically meaningful signal.
- **PSA density adds a second layer of risk stratification** — PSA concentration ÷ prostate volume from MRI; disproportionately elevated PSA relative to prostate size implicates disrupted architecture or leaky vasculature — both hallmarks of malignancy.
- **Biparametric MRI is now sufficient for pre-biopsy imaging** — the 2025 PRIME trial showed contrast-free biparametric MRI (no gadolinium injection) is noninferior to multiparametric MRI at half the scanning time, dramatically improving accessibility.
- **Transperineal biopsy achieves ~0% infection rate** — vs. 5–7% for the standard transrectal approach, while improving detection of high-grade tumors. Only 37.2% of US urologists currently offer it.
- **Active surveillance is appropriate for GG1–2 tumors** — low-grade prostate cancers can be monitored without treatment, avoiding side effects of premature intervention. Treatment is initiated only when monitoring shows escalation.
- **Finasteride masks PSA — correction factors are essential** — PSA is suppressed by ~50% within 12 months on a 5-ARI. Multiply raw PSA by 2x (years 1–2), 2.3x (years 2–7), 2.5x (years 7+). Failure to apply this has led to twice the rate of metastatic diagnosis in finasteride users.

## Key Words

**Prostate-Specific Antigen (PSA)** — a protein produced by the prostate to maintain seminal fluid liquidity; a small amount leaks into the bloodstream and is quantifiable by blood draw; elevated levels indicate prostate enlargement or pathology. *"PSA is a protein produced by the prostate to aid in maintaining the liquidity of seminal fluid. A small amount of PSA leaks through prostate vasculature into the blood."*

**PSA Velocity** — the rate of change in a patient's PSA over serial measurements; a sustained increase of >0.35 ng/mL/year (for baseline PSA <4.0 ng/mL) flags potential malignancy; threshold rises to >0.75 ng/mL/year for baseline PSA >4.0. *"By mapping an individual patient's baseline then tracking the rate of change through time, we get a value known as PSA Velocity."*

**PSA Density** — PSA blood concentration divided by prostate volume (measured via MRI); elevated density suggests PSA is leaking disproportionately, implicating disrupted tissue architecture or leaky vasculature, both hallmarks of malignant growth. *"If PSA levels in the blood are increasing significantly faster than the size of the prostate, it implicates increased PSA leakage from the prostate into the blood."*

**PLCO Trial** — the Prostate, Lung, Colorectal and Ovarian Cancer Screening Trial; the primary evidence base for the USPSTF's 2012 anti-screening guidelines; fatally contaminated because 39–64% of the "no screening" control group were actually undergoing PSA tests annually. *"The USPSTF's claim that screening saves at most 1.3 lives per 1,000 men screened is built on a study so irresponsibly reported that it is borderline unbelievable it passed the peer review process."*

**Biparametric MRI** — a contrast-free prostate MRI using only T2-weighted and diffusion-weighted imaging, without gadolinium contrast injection; shown noninferior to multiparametric MRI in the 2025 PRIME trial (29.2% vs. 29.6% clinically significant cancer detection), at half the scan time. *"The PRIME trial found that the contrast-free, biparametric MRI was noninferior to the gold standard."*

**Transperineal (TP) Biopsy** — a prostate biopsy approach entering through the perineum (skin between anus and scrotum), bypassing the rectum; achieves ~0% infection rate vs. 5–7% for the standard transrectal approach, while improving access to the full prostate and detection of high-grade tumors. *"Not a single patient undergoing TP biopsy developed an infection in this study."*

**Grade Group (GG)** — a 1–5 pathological grading scale applied to prostate tumor biopsies, reflecting how abnormal the cells appear relative to healthy tissue; GG1–2 qualify for active surveillance; GG3–5 indicate increasingly aggressive cancer requiring immediate treatment. *"A score of GG3, 4, or 5 indicates increasing proportions of increasingly malignant cells. This will typically lead to immediate treatment."*

**Active Surveillance** — a monitoring protocol for low-grade (GG1–2) prostate tumors; involves enhanced PSA testing, PHI/4Kscore blood tests, symptom tracking, and follow-up imaging; treatment is deferred until red flags emerge, avoiding unnecessary intervention for indolent disease. *"These patients are then put into active surveillance, but no treatment is initiated until further red flags are raised."*

**5-Alpha Reductase Inhibitor (5-ARI) / Finasteride** — a drug class that inhibits conversion of testosterone to the more potent androgen DHT; widely prescribed for hair loss; suppresses PSA by ~50% within 12 months, requiring multiplication of raw PSA values (2x at years 1–2, 2.3x at years 2–7, 2.5x at years 7+) to interpret screening results accurately. *"A PSA value obtained while on finasteride for 12 months or longer needs to be doubled, at least, to be accurately understood."*

**Metastatic Prostate Cancer (Stage IV)** — cancer that has spread beyond the prostate to other organs or tissue; 5-year survival drops from >99% at stages I–III to 38%, with a median survival of 30 months; represents nearly all prostate cancer deaths. *"More and more cases are being caught only once disease has become metastatic. At this point, prostate cancer is largely incurable."*

## Personal Takeaways

**PSA alone is an insufficient screening tool.** Since PSA values fluctuate day-to-day and differ considerably between individuals, a single measurement provides limited diagnostic signal. What matters is PSA velocity — the rate of change across serial measurements — which captures meaningful trends that a snapshot value cannot.

**PSA density adds a second dimension of risk.** By measuring prostate volume via MRI and relating it to the circulating PSA concentration, PSA density reveals whether PSA is leaking at a rate disproportionate to prostate size — a sign of disrupted tissue architecture or compromised vasculature, both hallmarks of malignancy.

**PSA testing is inexpensive and widely accessible**, making it a low-barrier entry point into screening — but only if interpreted within the right framework (velocity + density) rather than as a standalone value.

**Finasteride can artificially suppress PSA**, masking the true underlying level. Men taking the drug — commonly prescribed for male pattern hair loss — will show measured PSA values roughly half of what they would otherwise be. Without applying the appropriate correction factors, this leads to systematic underestimation of cancer risk.
