# Disappointing results from the first rapamycin-plus-exercise trial

**Author:** Taylor Yeater, Michael Rae, Peter Attia
**Type:** Article (peterattiamd.com)
**Published:** 2026-05-02

---

## Summary

The RAPA-EX-01 trial was the first randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled study to test weekly rapamycin (sirolimus) combined with an exercise program in older adults. Forty sedentary participants aged 65–85 were randomized to either 6 mg rapamycin or placebo once weekly for 13 weeks. Both groups performed the same home-based exercise program three times per week: body-weight chair-stands for resistance training and a stationary bike for endurance work. Rapamycin dosing was timed to Day 6 of each training week — approximately 24 hours after the last session — to avoid interfering with peak post-exercise anabolic signaling.

**Results.** Both groups improved with exercise. However, the rapamycin group improved less: approximately 2 fewer chair-stand repetitions over 30 seconds by week 13 (ITT adjusted mean difference: −2.13; 95% CI: −4.61–0.34; p=0.089 — not statistically significant under the prespecified primary analysis). The effect did reach significance in the prespecified sensitivity analyses: complete-case analysis (p=0.045) and per-protocol analysis (p=0.007). Secondary outcomes — 6-minute walk distance (−4.87 m, p=0.706), grip strength (−1.19 kg, p=0.344), and SF-36 quality-of-life score — all trended in the same direction, favoring placebo, though none individually reached significance. Safety signals included a statistically significant but small increase in HbA1c in the rapamycin group, and one case of pneumonia.

**The scientific rationale.** The trial was designed around the "cycling hypothesis": the idea that low-dose or intermittent rapamycin might spare — or even enhance — functional adaptations in older adults, while still delivering the autophagy-promoting geroprotective effects that make the drug interesting. The hypothesis was motivated by a paradox: mTORC1 signaling is counterintuitively *elevated* (not suppressed) in aging muscle, and individuals with lower basal mTOR signaling gain more lean mass from training. Inhibiting the age-related mTOR hyperactivity could therefore theoretically resolve anabolic resistance rather than worsen it.

**Attia's interpretation.** The article argues against declaring the case closed on rapamycin. Key objections: (1) The trial is small and the exercise stimulus was not rigorous — home-based, body-weight-only, not a standardized hypertrophy protocol. (2) More importantly, even a confirmed negative effect on muscle adaptation represents only one narrow outcome. The relevant question is whether any such impairment is large enough to outweigh rapamycin's potential benefits against the actual major killers — cardiovascular disease, cancer, neurodegeneration, metabolic disease — which this trial does not address at all. (3) A 13-week trial is structurally incapable of answering the geroprotection question; in animal models, rapamycin is studied over a large fraction of the lifespan. No short-term biomarkers can substitute. (4) The cycling hypothesis is not definitively falsified; a more rigorous hypertrophy stimulus and longer follow-up are needed.

Attia's bottom line: RAPA-EX-01 is a "small additional update in the negative direction on one narrow dimension." He remains cautiously interested in rapamycin but notes this study increases concern that dosing, timing, and context will matter greatly in practice. The trial was funded entirely by public donations with sample size capped by budget — which itself reinforces the case for larger, better-resourced studies.

## Key Ideas

- **The cycling hypothesis was the scientific basis for the trial** — alternating mTORC1 activation (anabolic window, during and after exercise) with mTORC1 inhibition (autophagic/geroprotective window, rapamycin dosing day) across the week, hoping to capture both benefits simultaneously.
- **mTOR signaling is paradoxically elevated in aging muscle** — contrary to what you might expect, age-related anabolic resistance does not result from suppressed mTOR; basal mTOR is overactive in older muscle, and individuals with *lower* basal mTOR signaling gain more lean mass from training.
- **The primary outcome missed significance under the prespecified analysis** — ITT p=0.089, direction consistently favoring placebo; per-protocol analysis did reach p=0.007, but is more vulnerable to bias as it excludes dropouts.
- **Consistent direction across all secondary outcomes** — walk distance, grip strength, and quality of life all trended toward placebo; the consistency raises the possibility this is a real signal, not noise.
- **Safety signals were unexpected under a cyclic protocol** — small HbA1c increase and one pneumonia case are biologically plausible but were not seen in Mannick's prior human studies using a similar protocol with a rapalog.
- **Muscle adaptation is not the primary target of geroprotection** — the diseases that overwhelmingly kill older adults are cardiovascular, oncologic, neurodegenerative, and metabolic; sarcopenia matters but must be weighed against these in a tradeoff framework.
- **This is fundamentally a tradeoff problem** — modest muscle adaptation impairment might be acceptable if rapamycin meaningfully reduces mortality risk across the major disease categories; unacceptable if it does not; this trial provides no information on the other side of that tradeoff.
- **13-week trials cannot answer geroprotection questions** — animal lifespan studies require a large fraction of the lifespan to show survival benefit; no short-term biomarker panel can substitute for that information.
- **The trial is credit-worthy despite its limitations** — it was a properly designed double-blind RCT on a drug too often discussed on the basis of animal data and n-of-1 enthusiasm; that it was funded entirely by public donations with a budget-capped sample size highlights the field's resource gap.
- **Precision will be required for any real-world rapamycin use** — dosing, timing relative to exercise, training intensity, and individual context will likely all matter; the current discourse underestimates this complexity.

## Key Words

**Rapamycin (Sirolimus)** — an mTOR inhibitor originally developed as an immunosuppressant for organ transplantation; studied as a potential geroprotector based on robust lifespan extension in multiple animal species; the drug at issue in RAPA-EX-01. *"Rapamycin inhibits mTORC1, a central signaling hub for protein synthesis, and thus muscular hypertrophy."*

**mTORC1 (Mechanistic Target of Rapamycin Complex 1)** — the primary molecular target of rapamycin; a central regulator of protein synthesis, autophagy, and cellular growth; elevated (not suppressed) in aging muscle, contributing paradoxically to anabolic resistance. *"Signaling through mTORC1 is paradoxically elevated in aging mouse and human muscle."*

**Geroprotector** — a drug or intervention hypothesized to slow the biology of aging itself, rather than treating a specific age-related disease; rapamycin is the leading pharmacological candidate based on animal data. *"A putative geroprotector."*

**Cycling Hypothesis** — the hypothesis that low-dose or intermittent rapamycin can spare or enhance functional (anabolic) adaptations in older adults, while still delivering autophagy-promoting geroprotective effects, by alternating mTORC1 activation and inhibition across the week. *"You might be able to separate the anabolic window from the catabolic-autophagic window, deliberately alternating mTORC1 activation and inhibition across the week."*

**Anabolic Resistance** — the age-related blunting of the muscle protein synthesis response to exercise and amino acids in older adults; paradoxically associated with elevated (not reduced) mTORC1 signaling; the target phenomenon the cycling hypothesis aims to correct. *"Exercise and amino acids trigger less muscle protein synthesis in older people's muscles than they do in younger people's."*

**Autophagy** — the cellular self-cleaning process by which damaged proteins and organelles are degraded and recycled; promoted by mTORC1 inhibition; one of the primary mechanisms through which rapamycin is thought to exert geroprotective effects. *"The autophagy-promoting, geroprotective effects that make the drug interesting in the first place."*

**Intention-to-Treat (ITT) Analysis** — a statistical approach that analyzes all randomized participants in the group they were assigned to, regardless of adherence or dropout; considered the most conservative and least biased primary analysis in an RCT. *"The data from all subjects who are randomized to either the drug or the placebo are analyzed."*

**Per-Protocol Analysis** — a sensitivity analysis that includes only participants who fully adhered to the trial protocol; more statistically powerful but more vulnerable to bias than ITT, since dropouts (often driven by side effects) are excluded. *"A per-protocol analysis (p=0.007), which included the most adherent of all the subjects in both groups."*

**Sarcopenia** — the age-related loss of skeletal muscle mass and strength; an important contributor to functional decline, falls, and mortality in older adults; the proximate concern motivating the RAPA-EX-01 trial design. *"Sarcopenia is clearly an important issue in aging."*

**HbA1c (Glycated Hemoglobin)** — a blood marker reflecting average blood glucose over approximately 3 months; elevated in the rapamycin group in this trial, consistent with the known risk of impaired glucose tolerance with mTOR inhibition; a safety signal to monitor in future trials.

**Hypertrophy** — the increase in muscle fiber size (cross-sectional area) in response to resistance training; blocked by high-dose continuous rapamycin; potentially spared or enhanced by intermittent low-dose rapamycin under the cycling hypothesis. *"High-dose, continuous rapamycin blocks the muscle-building response to overload."*

## Notable Quotes

> "RAPA-EX-01 represents a small additional update in the negative direction on one narrow dimension, namely muscle adaptation under these specific conditions, but it does not materially resolve the broader uncertainty around rapamycin's effects on the major drivers of aging and mortality."

> "This is fundamentally a tradeoff problem: a modest impairment in muscle adaptation might be acceptable if it comes with a meaningful reduction in cardiovascular, oncologic, or neurodegenerative risk, but clearly unacceptable if it does not."

> "A 13-week human trial is structurally incapable of informing those outcomes. No set of biomarkers measured over that time horizon is going to meaningfully resolve whether rapamycin slows cardiovascular, metabolic, neurodegenerative, or oncologic disease."

> "Until we have data from those trials, I remain where I've been on rapamycin: curious, cautiously interested, and nowhere near ready to close the book."
