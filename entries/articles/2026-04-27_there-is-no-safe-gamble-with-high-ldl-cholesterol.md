# There Is No Safe Gamble with High LDL Cholesterol

**Author:** Peter Attia, Tom Dayspring, Research Team
**Type:** Article
**Published:** 2026-04-25
**Started:** 2026-04-27

---

## Summary

*2026-04-27*

This article is a direct response to *The Cholesterol Code*, a documentary produced by the Citizen Science Foundation (CSF) that argues a subset of lean, metabolically healthy individuals — "lean mass hyper-responders" (LMHRs) — can develop very high LDL cholesterol (often 200–400+ mg/dL) on ketogenic diets without incurring the atherosclerotic risk that normally accompanies such levels. Attia and his team systematically dismantle both the supporting evidence and the proposed biological mechanism.

**The LMHR phenotype** is defined by a characteristic triad: LDL-C above 200 mg/dL, HDL-C above 80 mg/dL, and triglycerides below 70 mg/dL. The CSF's proposed explanation, the "lipid energy model," holds that carbohydrate restriction depletes liver glycogen, increases lipolysis and NEFA flux to the liver, raises VLDL secretion, and through rapid lipoprotein lipase–mediated VLDL turnover ultimately produces elevated LDL-C. The model interprets this as adaptive fat metabolism rather than pathology.

**Problems with the evidence:** The primary LMHR study — a one-year prospective cohort of 100 individuals using coronary CT angiography (CCTA) — found no association between apoB, LDL-C, and plaque progression over one year. The article argues this result is structurally uninformative: atherosclerosis is the product of cumulative apoB exposure over decades, not a single year. The expected signal cannot appear in such a short window in a relatively young, low-burden cohort. A companion cross-sectional study comparing 80 LMHR individuals to matched controls from the Miami Heart cohort showed no difference in coronary plaque burden — but the matching was deeply confounded. The KETO group was leaner, had lower inflammation (hsCRP 0.5 vs. 0.7 mg/L), higher HDL, and lower triglycerides. These accumulated advantages, not LDL-C per se, most plausibly explain any apparent equivalence.

**Problems with the mechanism:** The lipid energy model cannot account for the observed magnitude of apoB elevation. Increased hepatic triglyceride synthesis primarily enlarges VLDL particles, it does not increase particle number — which is what drives atherogenesis. If more VLDL particles were being secreted, plasma triglycerides would be elevated in steady state, directly contradicting the low-TG hallmark of the phenotype. Furthermore, the proposed VLDL-to-LDL conversion via lipoprotein lipase has never been measured or validated in this population, despite assays existing to do so.

**The critical question** is not how or why apoB is elevated — it is whether sustained, markedly elevated apoB-containing lipoprotein exposure causes atherosclerosis regardless of the metabolic context producing it. Decades of converging evidence — Mendelian randomization studies, prospective cohort studies, and randomized controlled trials of LDL-lowering therapies — consistently demonstrate that apoB drives atherosclerosis, and that reducing it reduces risk, independent of the mechanism. Familial hypercholesterolemia provides the most instructive parallel: FH patients also feel healthy for decades, often with normal CTAs, and don't typically suffer major cardiac events until their third or fourth decade of life. Absence of early detectable signal is not evidence of absence of harm — it is the expected pattern under a fully causal model.

**The practical conclusion** is asymmetric: the cost of treating elevated LDL-C in an LMHR (medication, dietary modification) is modest and well tolerated; the cost of being wrong and not treating is irreversible atherosclerotic disease. Until prospective, decades-long data with proper control groups demonstrate safety, high LDL-C remains a risk to manage, not a curiosity to rationalize.

## Key Ideas

*2026-04-27*

- **ApoB drives atherosclerosis universally** — hundreds of population, genetic, and interventional studies converge on this. The mechanism producing elevated apoB doesn't change its atherogenicity; the arterial wall responds to particle number and exposure time, not metabolic origin.
- **The LMHR phenotype is real; its safety is not established** — the triad (very high LDL-C, very high HDL-C, low TG) in lean ketogenic dieters does appear to exist, but no evidence shows these individuals are exempt from ASCVD; it simply hasn't had time to develop.
- **One year is structurally insufficient** — atherosclerosis is a decades-long cumulative process; a one-year study in a young, low-burden cohort is expected to show no association under a fully causal model; null short-term findings are not evidence against causality.
- **Healthy user bias ruins the companion study** — the KETO group was systematically healthier across many variables (BMI, inflammation, HDL, TG) not captured by the matching criteria; any apparent cardiovascular equivalence is better explained by overall health than by LDL-C being harmless.
- **The lipid energy model fails mechanistically** — increased hepatic fat synthesis expands VLDL size, not particle number; high VLDL particle numbers would predict high plasma TG (contradicted by the phenotype); VLDL-to-LDL conversion has never been measured in LMHRs.
- **Familial hypercholesterolemia is the instructive parallel** — FH patients are metabolically distinct from LMHRs, yet the FH experience demonstrates that decades of apoB exposure cause massive ASCVD even in individuals who appear and feel well for years; the LMHR data horizon is simply too short.
- **Risk asymmetry argues for treatment** — tools to lower apoB (statins, ezetimibe, PCSK9 inhibitors) are effective and well tolerated; the expected-value calculation favors reducing exposure rather than betting on unproven exemption.
- **Metabolic health matters, but it's not a free pass** — being lean, insulin-sensitive, and physically active reduces ASCVD risk meaningfully, but LDL-C remains independently associated with ASCVD even in individuals with the most favorable metabolic profiles.

## Key Words

*2026-04-27*

**Apolipoprotein B (ApoB)** — a protein that wraps atherogenic lipoprotein particles (LDL, VLDL, IDL, Lp(a)); because each particle carries exactly one apoB molecule, plasma apoB concentration directly measures the number of atherogenic particles in circulation — a more accurate risk marker than LDL-C alone. *"Plasma apoB level essentially serves as a measure of the number of LDL particles, which in turn closely approximates the total number of atherogenic lipoproteins in circulation."*

**LDL Cholesterol (LDL-C)** — the mass of cholesterol carried collectively by LDL particles; historically the primary screening metric; less accurate than apoB when particle number and cholesterol cargo are dissociated (as in LMHRs). *"The problem with this approach is that the relationship between LDL-C and LDL particle number is skewed in a significant fraction of the population, such that LDL-C can under- or overestimate true risk."*

**Lean Mass Hyper-Responder (LMHR)** — a phenotype proposed by the CSF: individuals who, on a low-carbohydrate or ketogenic diet, develop very high LDL-C (often >200 mg/dL), very high HDL-C (>80 mg/dL), and low triglycerides (<70 mg/dL), while remaining lean, physically active, and metabolically healthy by conventional markers. *"Their LDL-C rises dramatically, often exceeding 200 mg/dL and sometimes reaching levels above 400 mg/dL."*

**Lipid Energy Model** — the CSF's proposed mechanism for LMHR: carbohydrate restriction depletes liver glycogen → increased lipolysis and NEFA flux → elevated hepatic triglyceride synthesis → increased VLDL secretion → rapid LPL-mediated VLDL turnover → elevated LDL-C; the article argues this model fails to explain particle number elevation and contradicts the low-TG hallmark. *"The lipid energy model… proposes a complex cascade of metabolic adaptations in response to carbohydrate restriction."*

**Atherosclerotic Cardiovascular Disease (ASCVD)** — the cumulative buildup of apoB-containing lipoprotein particles in arterial walls, initiating an inflammatory response and plaque formation; characterized by being slow, silent, and cumulative — driven by the integral of apoB concentration over time, not point-in-time measurements. *"Atherosclerotic risk is a function of cumulative exposure to apoB-containing lipoproteins, often conceptualized as the integral of apoB concentration over time."*

**Familial Hypercholesterolemia (FH)** — a genetic condition causing extremely elevated LDL-C from birth due to defective LDL receptor function; provides the strongest human evidence for apoB causality; FH patients develop severe ASCVD despite often feeling well for decades. *"Heterozygous FH patients typically do not experience major adverse cardiac events until their third or fourth decade, and even children with homozygous FH… usually do not suffer adverse events for a decade or more after birth."*

**Mendelian Randomization** — a genetic epidemiology method that uses naturally occurring genetic variants affecting a risk factor (e.g., LDL-C) as proxies for lifetime exposure, free from the confounders of observational studies; consistently shows LDL-C and apoB are causally related to ASCVD. *"These conclusions are supported by (literally) hundreds of population studies, genetic studies such as Mendelian randomization, randomized controlled trials of LDL-lowering medications, and basic science research."*

**Lipoprotein Lipase (LPL)** — an enzyme that removes triglycerides from VLDL particles in peripheral tissues, converting them to smaller, denser LDL particles; the LMHR model proposes elevated LPL-mediated VLDL turnover as the driver of high LDL-C in this phenotype, but this conversion has never been measured in LMHRs. *"Advocates of the LMHR hypothesis have produced no evidence to date that LMHRs exhibit high rates of VLDL lipolysis or elevated lipoprotein lipase activity."*

**Healthy User Bias** — a systematic confounding effect in observational studies where individuals who voluntarily adopt and adhere to health-promoting behaviors (e.g., strict ketogenic diet for years) differ from the general population across many lifestyle variables not captured by standard matching criteria, making them appear metabolically favorable independent of the intervention being studied. *"People who voluntarily maintain a strict ketogenic diet for nearly five years likely differ significantly from the general population in physical activity, socioeconomic status, overall diet quality, and many other lifestyle factors."*
